\section{Data Acquisition}
One of the primary challenges in training deep learning models for \ac{TCG} identification is the problem of \textit{data scarcity}. While the total number of unique cards in circulation is vast (often exceeding tens of thousands for games like \ii{Magic: The Gathering}), labeled datasets containing real-world photographs of these cards are virtually non-existent. Collecting and manually labeling a sufficiently large dataset of physical cards is prohibitively expensive and time-consuming.

To address this limitation, this thesis adopts a \textit{synthetic data generation} approach. The ground truth \textit{template} images were aggregated from the Scryfall Bulk Data API, which provides a comprehensive JSON catalogue of all printed cards.

\subsection{Scraping Pipeline and Indexing}
Custom data acquisition script, \texttt{data\_scraper.py}, was developed to process the metadata and download high-resolution scans. A critical aspect of the pipeline is the \ii{file indexing strategy}, designed to balance machine uniqueness with human readability, a feature essential for manual inspection and debugging. % TODO: be more descriptive about where the source code is located? scripts/data_scraper.py

Instead of relying solely on human-unreadable identifiers (such as \acp{UUID}), a hybrid naming convetion derived from the card's canonical \ac{URI} is proposed. Each file name combines the card edition, collector number, and name with a \ac{UUID}: 
\begin{equation}
    \texttt{filename} = \texttt{edition}\_\texttt{number}\_\texttt{name}\text{-}\texttt{UUID}.\texttt{png}
\end{equation}
For example, a card from the \textit{Zendikar} set is stored as:
\begin{center}
    \texttt{zen\_21\_kor-outfitter-00006596-1166-4a79-8443-ca9f82e6db4e.png}
\end{center}
This format allows for immediate visual identification of the card's edition and variant without requiring a lookup table, while the \ac{UUID} suffix guarantees collision-free storage even for edge cases (e.g., duplicate names across sets).

\subsection{Handling Complex Layouts}
The script also implements logic to handle complex card layouts, such as \acp{DFC} (transform, modal \acp{DFC}). These entries, identified by the \texttt{layout} property in the JSON metadata, contain multiple faces within a single object. Our pipeline detects these instances and automatically splits them into separate template images, appending \texttt{\_front} or \texttt{\_back} suffixes to their identifiers to ensure that both sides of the card are learnable by the model.

Rate limiting and error handling (using \texttt{wget} with retries) were implemented to ensure stability during the bulk download of tens of thousands of assets.

\section{Synthetic Generation}
Since manual annotation is infeasible, synthetic training samples are generated on the fly. The augmentation pipeline, implemented in \texttt{train.py}, simulates the imperfections of physical card scanning.

For every training epoch, the \texttt{MTGOnlineDataset} class loads a pristine digital scan and applies a randomized composition of transformations defined in the \texttt{get\_transforms} function. Key augmentations include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Geometric Distortions: Perspective transformations and rotation (limit $15^{\circ}$) mimic the camera angle and misalignment.
    \item Photometric Noise: Color jitter (brightness, contrast, saturation) and Gaussian blur simulate varying lighting conditions and camera focus issues.
    \item Occlusions: Using \texttt{CoarseDropout}, we simulate minor physical defects or dust on the card surface.
\end{itemize}
This approach exposes the model to a diverse range of tensors, effectively minimizing the likelihood of repetition, although it does not entirely eliminate the possibility.

\section{Model Architecture}
