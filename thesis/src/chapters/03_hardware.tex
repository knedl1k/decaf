\chapter{Hardware Design}\label{chap:hw}

As mentioned in \refsec{sec:commercial_solutions}, existing solutions on the market are difficult to modify and are closed-source. The architecture described in this chapter therefore represents the first version of an open-source prototype. Its primary goal is to verify the feasibility of automated scanning of \ac{TCG} cards using commonly available hardware. Thanks to its intentionally simple mechanical design, the platform provides a foundation that is easy to reproduce and modify. Advanced features (such as controlled LED lighting panels, sorting mechanisms with multiple hoppers, or motorized high-capacity feeders) are left for future iterations.

The system does not rely on proprietary industrial equipment but is primarily assembled from standard commercial components combined with custom 3D-printed parts.

\section{System Architecture Overview}
The system functions as a simple \ii{pick-and-place} mechanism that uses a pneumatic end-effector. The architecture is divided into three primary subsystems:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Pneumatic Circuit: enables card handling.
    \item Electromechanical actuation: moves the robotic arm.
    \item Compute and Control: robot movement, card capture, recognition and classification.
\end{enumerate}
To minimize production costs, all structural joints, support frames, card holders, and the camera mount were manufactured using 3D printing.

\section{Pneumatic Handling System}\label{sec:pneumatic_handling}
The physical manipulation of \ac{TCG} paper cards requires an end-effector capable of establishing a secure grip without inducing mechanical stress, bending and/or surface abrasion. As previously validated by Yu~\cite{pokerrobot} in the context of a robotic poker dealer (see \Cref{yolo-studies}), vacuum-based suction mechanisms provide a highly effective solution for the non-destructive handling of thin card stock. 

Building upon this premise, our system utilizes a pneumatic circuit powered by a Sparmax TC-610H Plus compressor. Sourced from existing laboratory inventory, this specific model provides continuous airflow. And thanks to its integrated air tank, all pressure pulsations are eliminated; ensuring a perfectly stable vacuum state during the robotic transfer process.

\subsection{Vacuum Generation}\label{subsec:venturi}
Instead of integrating a dedicated, electromechanical vacuum pump, the vacuum is generated purely pneumatically utilizing the Venturi effect\footnote{The Venturi effect is a principle of fluid dynamics establishing that as an incompressible fluid passes through a constricted section of a pipe, its velocity must increase, leading to a corresponding drop in static pressure. This localized low pressure is utilized to draw in external air, effectively creating a vacuum.}. 

A custom Venturi ejector, visualized in \Cref{fig:venturi_render}, was designed and 3D-printed specifically for this platform. Detailed mechanical dimensions and exact nozzle specifications are provided in \Cref{app:venturi}.

To mitigate the acoustic noise generated by the high-velocity exhaust air, a standard M5 pneumatic muffler is integrated into the exhaust port of the generator.

\subsection{Actuation and End-Effector}\label{subsec:airflow}
The airflow routing and timing are controlled by a 3/2 normally closed (NC) solenoid valve operating at 24~V DC. The valve acts as the primary logical switch for the pneumatic circuit; when energized, compressed air flows to the Venturi generator, instantly inducing a vacuum at the end-effector. When de-energized, the system vents to the atmosphere, releasing the card. 

The end-effector itself utilizes commercially available silicone suction cup with diameter of 15.5~mm. Silicone was selected for its high flexibility and non-marking properties, which are critical to preventing damage to the glossy finishes commonly found on \ac{TCG} cards. The pneumatic components are connected using flexible 6/4 mm polyurethane tubing, routed alongside the electromechanical arm to prevent tangling during operation.
