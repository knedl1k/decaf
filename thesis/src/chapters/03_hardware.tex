\chapter{Hardware Design}\label{chap:hw}

As mentioned in \Cref{sec:commercial_solutions}, existing solutions on the market are difficult to modify and are closed-source. The architecture described in this chapter therefore represents the first version of an open-source prototype. Its primary goal is to verify the feasibility of automated scanning of \ac{TCG} cards using commonly available hardware. Thanks to its intentionally simple mechanical design, the platform provides a foundation that is easy to reproduce and modify. Advanced features (such as controlled LED lighting panels, sorting mechanisms with multiple hoppers, or motorized high-capacity feeders) are left for future iterations.

\section{System Architecture Overview}
The system functions as a simple \ii{pick-and-place} mechanism that uses a pneumatic end-effector. The architecture is divided into three primary subsystems:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \ii{Pneumatic Circuit}: enables card handling.
    \item \ii{Electromechanical actuation}: moves the robotic arm.
    \item \ii{Compute and Control}: robot movement, card capture, recognition and classification.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Pneumatic Handling System}\label{sec:pneumatic_handling}
The physical manipulation of \ac{TCG} paper cards requires an end-effector capable of establishing a secure grip without inducing mechanical stress, bending and/or surface abrasion. As previously validated by Yu~\cite{yu2023pokerrobot} in the context of a robotic poker dealer (see \Cref{yolo-studies}), vacuum-based suction mechanisms provide a highly effective solution for the non-destructive handling of thin card stock. 

Building upon this premise, our system uses a pneumatic circuit powered by a Sparmax TC-610H Plus compressor. Thanks to its integrated air tank, all pressure pulsations are eliminated; ensuring a perfectly stable vacuum state during the robotic transfer process.
\newpage
\subsection{Actuation and End-Effector}\label{subsec:airflow}
The airflow routing and timing are controlled by a 3/2 normally closed (NC) solenoid valve operating at 24\,V DC. The valve acts as the primary logical switch for the pneumatic circuit; when turned on, compressed air flows to the \hyperref[subsec:venturi]{Venturi generator}, creating a vacuum at the end-effector. When turned off, the system vents to the atmosphere, releasing the card. 
\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.4\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{./fig/schematics/valve-crop.pdf}
    \caption{Schematic of the MOSFET switching circuit used to control the 24\,V solenoid valve via the Arduino.}
    \label{fig:scheme_valve}
    
    \vspace{2em}
    
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{./fig/sketch/venturi_transparent.png}
    \caption{Render of the 3D-printed Venturi vacuum generator. Designed by Nikoś~\cite{venturi_model}.}
    \label{fig:venturi_render}
    
    \vspace{-4em}
\end{wrapfigure}

The circuit uses an IRLZ34N, a logic-level N-channel MOSFET, which can be fully saturated by the 5\,V signal from the Arduino's digital pin (\texttt{D6}). A 220\,$\Omega$ series resistor (\texttt{R1}) limits the inrush current to the MOSFET's gate capacitor, protecting the microcontroller pin. A 10\,k$\Omega$ pull-down resistor (\texttt{R2}) guarantees that the gate is held securely at ground potential during the system boot sequence, preventing spurious actuation of the valve while the Arduino pins are in a floating state. 

To protect the MOSFET from the destructive back-EMF voltage spikes\footnote{When the current flowing through an inductive load (such as a solenoid) is abruptly interrupted, the rapidly collapsing magnetic field induces a high-voltage transient across the switch. If unmitigated, this reverse voltage spike can significantly exceed the maximum drain-to-source rating of the MOSFET, leading to avalanche breakdown and permanent component failure~\cite{mohan2003power}.} generated by the solenoid's collapsing magnetic field, flyback protection is handled directly at the hardware interface. The valve is connected using a Hirschmann GML 209 NJ DIN connector, which features an integrated freewheeling diode, a varistor and an LED status indicator. This solution safely suppresses voltage transients and provides immediate visual diagnostic feedback without requiring additional discrete components on the control board.

The end-effector itself uses commercially available silicone suction cup with diameter of 15.5\,mm. Silicone was selected for its high flexibility. The pneumatic components are connected using flexible 6/4 mm polyurethane tubing, routed alongside the 3D-printed arm to prevent tangling during operation.

\subsection{Vacuum Generation}\label{subsec:venturi}
The vacuum is produced using a 3D-printed Venturi\footnote{The Venturi effect is a principle of fluid dynamics establishing that as an incompressible fluid passes through a constricted section of a pipe, its velocity must increase, leading to a corresponding drop in static pressure. This localized low pressure is used to draw in external air, effectively creating a vacuum.}  ejector, visualized in \Cref{fig:venturi_render}. To achieve this, an open-source model designed by Nikoś~\cite{venturi_model} was used without modification.

To mitigate the acoustic noise generated by the high-velocity exhaust air, a standard M5 pneumatic muffler is integrated into the exhaust port of the generator.

\section{Electromechanical Actuation}\label{sec:electromech}

\subsection{Main Axis Control}\label{subsec:main_axis}
\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.4\textwidth}
    \centering
    \vspace{-1em}
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{./fig/schematics/encoder-crop.pdf}
    \caption{Wiring schematic for the AS5147 magnetic rotary encoder.}
    \label{fig:encoder_circuit}
\end{wrapfigure}
The primary horizontal movement of the arm is driven by a 12--24\,V bipolar stepper motor. While stepper motors provide excellent open-loop positional accuracy, operating them in an open-loop configuration carries the risk of undetected step-loss due to mechanical resonance or unexpected physical collisions. To mitigate this vulnerability, an AS5147 magnetic rotary encoder is coupled to the main axis, as detailed in \Cref{fig:encoder_circuit}.

The motor is controlled via a TMC2209 stepper driver. Rather than relying on specialized industrial motor controllers, it was selected due to its status as a ubiquitous standard within the open-source hardware and 3D printing communities. Its widespread market availability and low cost ensures that the robotic platform remains easily reproducible for future users. TMC2209 also provides smooth microstepping out-of-the-box, which naturally helps minimize mechanical vibrations during the arm's transit without requiring complex low-level tuning. The logical integration between the microcontroller and the stepper driver is illustrated in \Cref{fig:tmc2209_circuit}.

\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.5\textwidth}
    \centering
    \vspace{-1em}
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{./fig/schematics/driver-crop.pdf}
    \caption{Wiring schematic for the TMC2209 stepper motor driver.}
    \label{fig:tmc2209_circuit}
\end{wrapfigure}
To maximize software reliability and minimize the complexity of the control loop, the driver is deployed in a hardware-configured standalone mode. As shown in the schematic, the digital interface with the Arduino Uno uses a standard two-wire control protocol (\texttt{STEP} and \texttt{DIR}). The active-low \texttt{\char`~EN} (Enable) pin is intentionally left unconnected\footnote{Due to the driver's internal pull-down resistor, leaving the \texttt{\char`~EN} pin floating keeps the motor coils permanently energized. This behavior is highly beneficial for the robotic platform, as it provides continuous holding torque, preventing any unwanted mechanical drifting of the arm during periods of inactivity. Also, actively toggling the disable state on this specific breakout board requires a dedicated 5\,V supply line rather than a standard microcontroller GPIO signal, making the hardware-level bypass the more practical design choice.}. The \texttt{DIR} (Direction) and \texttt{STEP} pins define the rotational vector and the precise movement increments, respectively.

By intentionally leaving the \texttt{UART} and configuration pins unconnected, the system avoids the overhead of serial configuration protocols. Instead, it relies inherently on the driver's default behavior, effectively abstracting the complex motor phase calculations away from the microcontroller. Finally, to ensure strict galvanic isolation of the logic signals, the driver separates its power domains: the high-current motor phases are supplied by the 24\,V circuit (\texttt{VS}), while the internal logic is safely referenced to the Arduino's 5\,V rail (\texttt{VCC\_IO}).

\newpage
\subsection{Z-Axis Mechanism}\label{subsec:z_axis}
\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.4\textwidth}
    \vspace{-1em}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{./fig/schematics/servo-crop.pdf}
    \caption{Wiring schematic for the MG90S servo.}
    \label{fig:servo_circuit}
\end{wrapfigure}
To pick up and place the cards, a secondary linear vertical axis (Z-axis) is mounted at the distal end of the main arm. Given that the payload consists solely of a single piece of card stock and lightweight silicone suction cups, deploying a traditional stepper motor and lead screw would introduce unnecessary inertial mass. 

Instead, an MG90S micro servo is used to drive a custom 3D-printed linear mechanism that lowers and raises the pneumatic end-effector. The power-to-weight ratio of the micro servo minimizes the dynamic load on the main axis. This weight reduction translates to higher achievable accelerations during horizontal travel, which increases the overall sorting throughput.

\section{Control and Vision Hardware}\label{sec:control_vision}
The computational architecture uses a master-slave configuration, physically decoupling high-level asynchronous processing from deterministic, real-time hardware execution. The detailed software implementation and communication protocols are defined in \Cref{chap:sw}.

\subsection{Vision and High-Level Logic}\label{subsec:rpi}
The high-level orchestration, including the computer vision pipeline, is hosted on a \ac{SBC}, specifically a Raspberry Pi 3B (1\,GB RAM). As the robotic arm transfers a card to the inspection stage, an image is captured using a Raspberry Pi Camera Module v2.1. 

\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.4\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{./fig/sketch/rpicamera_mount.png}
    \caption{Render of the 3D-printed RPi camera mount.}
    \label{fig:rpi_mount}
    \vspace{-1em}
\end{wrapfigure}
The camera is securely mounted on a 3D-printed bracket (see \Cref{fig:rpi_mount}). This fixed mechanical positioning ensures a consistent focal length and a similar viewing angle, thereby minimizing perspective distortion and actively mitigating the \ii{domain shift} between simulation and reality, as discussed in \Cref{sec:domain_shift}. The captured image is then processed entirely on the \ac{SBC}, as illustrated in \Cref{fig:pipeline_flow}. 

\subsection{Electromechanical Control}\label{subsec:arduino}
While the Raspberry Pi offers significant computational capacity, its standard GNU/Linux operating system cannot guarantee the strict, microsecond-level real-time execution required for smooth stepper motor actuation. Therefore, low-level physical control is delegated to an Arduino Uno microcontroller operating as the slave device. 

The Arduino is physically connected to the Raspberry Pi via a standard USB interface, over which it receives positional targets and system triggers using serial communication. The Arduino was selected for this initial prototype due to its native 5\,V logic level, as well as its widespread availability and proven reliability within the open-source hardware community. 

Operating independently of the \acp{SBC} processing load, the Arduino is exclusively responsible for generating the precise, uninterrupted step pulses for the TMC2209 driver, managing the Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) signals for the Z-axis servo and instantaneously actuating the 24\,V solenoid valve via the dedicated MOSFET circuit to ensure drop-timing accuracy.

\section{Structural Design and Assembly}\label{sec:structs}
\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.5\textwidth}
    \vspace{-1em}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth, keepaspectratio]{./fig/photos/assembly.jpg}
    \caption{A photo of the assembled prototype.}
    \label{fig:assembly}
\end{wrapfigure}
To ensure the hardware platform remains accessible and easily replicable by the broader open-source community, all structural components were designed parametrically for 3D printing. The base chassis, camera mount, card hoppers and card trays are manufactured from standard PLA filament.

The mechanical assembly relies on modularity and serviceability. Components are secured with standard metric machine screws (M3 and M4) in conjunction with brass heat-set threaded inserts. Melting these inserts directly into the printed plastic provides significantly superior pull-out strength and vibration resistance compared to tapping threads directly into the polymer.

See \Cref{fig:assembly} for the assembled prototype.
